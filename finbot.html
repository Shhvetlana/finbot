Built my own family finance bot.
Here's why no existing tool worked.

Svetlana Egorova

When we bought a house in France, our spreadsheets stopped working. Not because spreadsheets are bad, but because we suddenly had two countries, six banks, three currencies, and statements arriving in Armenian and Hebrew. I needed slices of data that the tables couldn't hold anymore.

I looked for an existing tool. Searched in Russian and English, and tested everything that came up. Not a single one could ingest statements from all my banks. The language barrier wasn't a minor inconvenience — it was a hard wall. Armenian PDFs, Hebrew spreadsheets with no card-level detail, Israeli CSVs with aggregated settlements. No budgeting app was built for this combination.

So I built my own. And the search I made wasn't wasted — I catalogued every feature I'd seen across the market, and that list became my backlog.

01

The first real decision was behavioural

A tool only works if both people actually use it. My husband and I live in Telegram: it's where we talk, coordinate, and share things. Adding a separate app would mean remembering to open it, and that extra step kills the habit before it starts. I set up a dedicated group with a bot instead. It's pinned and always there. Logging an expense feels like sending a message, because it is.

02

The second tension was about data

Bank statements are sensitive; uploading them to someone else's app means trusting their infrastructure, their retention policy, and their breach response. Building my own system meant I controlled where the data lived and who could see it. I realised that Telegram holds all uploaded files, so statements uploaded through a web form are parsed regardless of language or format, reconciled and normalised into a single database with no sensitive information.

03

Under the hood

The bank statements are enriched with spending notes and normalised into DWH-level views. Between uploads, you type or photograph a receipt, and the AI extracts the rest. Once a month, a full report comes back — budget vs. actuals, forecast, runway in months, unusual merchants, behavioural patterns by category and day of week.

04

Making something frictionless creates its own risk

A system that flags everything unusual stops being read. I had to tune carefully: catch forgotten subscriptions and suspicious patterns, without crying wolf on every unfamiliar merchant. The first real win — finding 3 subscriptions we were certain we were tracking — only landed because the signal wasn't buried in noise.

05

The hardest architectural lesson: AI is not a calculator

Early versions had the model doing financial reasoning directly — it produced fluent, confident, occasionally wrong numbers. The fix was structural: all calculations happen in SQL views or Python code first. The AI receives pre-computed facts and writes conclusions on top of them. Fifteen prompt iterations, each validated against hand-labelled reference outputs, before anything went into production.

06

On using multiple models

Different AI models have different strengths — GPT helped me think through database structure, Claude was better at bot behaviour and prompt tuning. Using both, in fresh chats, with a reference output to check against, made debugging faster than any single tool would have.

07

Documentation as continuity

After every meaningful change, write it down. Detailed documentation isn't overhead — it's what lets you continue in a new chat, with a new model, without losing context. Fifteen versions of documentation kept the project coherent across days of iteration.

When finances are transparent to both people, the anxiety around them drops. We started making purchases more deliberately — and somehow that gave us more shared evenings, even with completely different daily food schedules. Awareness of what we spend on food turned into actual dinners together.

Built for our specific chaos. If yours looks similar, I'm happy to talk through what it would take to set it up.
